Case: GameStop Short Squeeze

r/wallstreetbets - GameStop Short Squeeze

  • Early 2021: a “short squeeze” organised by Redditors costs hedgefund investors who bet against GameStop billions of dollars
  • “What’s being accomplished now is what Occupy Wall Street tried and failed to do—a power shift, a shift in some control from Wall Street to Main Street.” – Jaime Rogozinski, founder of r/wallstreetbets

  • January 2021: a short squeeze largely organized through the subreddit /wallstreetbets inflicts high costs on prominent hedgefund investors like Melvin capital
  • in the wake of the events, many compare the actions of the subreddit to the occupy wall street movement
  • And indeed, recruitment and mobilisation in the subreddit was impressive as it gathered over 2 million new members over a 7 day period
  • the damage for Wallstreet investors would probably been even larger if at one point the trading platform Robinhood hadn’t prevented people from buying even more shares. The company behind Robinhood has financial ties to the large investment firms Redditors inflicted financial damage on

What is Shorting?

  • But before we get into our analysis, I feel obligated to at least quickly cover what a short squeeze is and what exactly happened
  • Here you see the development of the Gamestop stock over time
  • Many investors believed that it was overvalued by 2020 and would go further down or even go bankrupt
  • Gamestop’s main business model is selling physical copies of videogames and these are increasingly sold as digital licences online. Additionally GameStop is a brick and mortar business, which generally suffered with the outbreak of the pandemic
  • So many investors wanted to make money off this seemingly dying business. But, how do you do that?

What is Shorting?

  • traditionally: buy stocks at a low price and sell at a high price so you can keep the difference
  • so you buy at 18.84$ on jan fist 2021 and if the price goes up to 23.84, you make 5 bucks
  • When you short, you do the opposite: you borrow a stock and immediately sell it for 18.84$
  • if the value of the Gamestop stock falls to 13.84, you make 5 bucks
  • now interesting, when the stock gains in value, you loose money and the higher the price by the time you need to return the borrowed stock, the higher your losses

What Happened with the GameStop Stock?

  • So then what happened in 2021
  • Some Redditors had noticed that hedgefunds had borrowed more shares of Gamestop than actually existed at the time, and they know Melvin capital and the other big firms would need to buy these stocks by the deadline at whatever they cost at this point
  • So they bought and then hold as many of these shares as they could
  • At the highest point, investors who sold Gamestop short would have made a loss of $ 464.16 per share – much more than what the stock was worth at the beginning of the year

Theory

So then how do we make sense of that? we combine four pieces of theory, which I want to go over quickly: - Connective Action - Platform Power - Platform affordances - Professional norms and practices of journalism

Connective Action Framework

  • Bennett and Segerberg (2013)
  • Contrast to traditional collective action frames: symbolic inclusiveness and technological openness
  • Occupy Wall Street slogan “we are the 99%” represented a personal action frame

The first one is the connective action framework by Bennett and Segerberg:

  • they argued that brick and mortar organizations were replaced by Connective Action in the indignados and Occupy Wall Street protest movements
  • they argue that the framing “we are the 99%” drew people in while the slogan spread through social media platforms Tumblr, Twitter, and Facebook.
  • Now why are we looking at connective action in the Gamestop case? Because many have claimed that the short squeeze was the spiritual succesor of the OWS, movement. Only instead of discoursive power, Redditors used their combined financial power to attack Wallstreet
  • However, we argue that WallStreetBets was neither symbolically inclusive nor technologically open, but shows how the media system has developed even beyond what Bennett and Segerberg saw in 2011

Platform Power & Platform affordances

  • This leads us to the second and third concept: Platform Power and Platform affordances
  • Now this case clearly demonstrates how platforms have become and important part of our physical lives
  • However, platforms are often lumped together as some argue that they follow one “social media logic”
  • In the Gamsetop short squeeze, this was clearly not case as the WallstreetBets subreddit has the rule that users should not post “political bullshit”,

Platform affordances

Reddit Twitter
Ties between users Clearer social group boundaries demarcating the subreddit as a “community” Weaker network ties created through hashtags, which suggest mutual interest but not shared identity
Moderation A clear set of rules specific to the subreddit (e.g. “no political bullshit”) which is primarily enforced by group moderators No moderation apart from platform action against extreme / illegal content
Selection mechanisms Upvoting: used primarily to establish the credibility of users and content Algorithmic sorting based on likes + retweets + unknown other variables
Language Monolingual (at the subreddit level, which is then also a feature of the platform infrastructure) Multilingual
  • So diving a little deeper into the platform affordances concept, we can distiguish between the things that are common on Reddit and Twitter
  • By the way, we define affordances as the sum of what technologies allow people to do and the norms and practices users built on top of that

Based on that, we identified a few key differences in the literature:

  • Reddit is more oriented toward community-building through stronger social ties, and the coordination of action

  • Twitter is more open and relies on weak or weaker ties

  • each subreddit has moderators, while there is no moderation on Twitter (apart from illegal content)

  • on subreddits, norms are also enforced via an upvote and downvote system where users determine what is relevant and interesting, while the selection mechanism on Twitter is user engagement + the proprietary algorithm used by Titter

  • This could well explain the differently shaped discussions on the platforms of which we saw some examples above.

  • Overall, we expect more political discussions on Twitter, where we should also see that new people are exposed to the short squeeze

  • While Reddit is probably more targeted towards mobilisation for a common cause, in this case making money with the stock market

Professional norms and practices of journalism

  • Mainstream media is influenced by elite framing, as indicated by theories of indexing and cascading activation (e.g., Bell & Entman, 2011).
  • Pressures of the news media business model contributes economic coverage which is ideologically narrower and even more oriented toward elite framing (Davis, 2018; Guardino, 2019).
  • Business and financial news reporters are broadly too dependent on the knowledge and framing of insiders and often share outlook and interpretation of the insiders they are supposed to be reporting on (Schiffrin, 2015; Stiglitz, 2015).
  • finally, there is already tons of research on how legacy news media reporting is shaped by professional norms and practices of journalism
  • So we expect that most reporting of the short squeeze will fall in line with what elites, and in this case Wall Street insiders, say about the events

Research Questions

  1. To what extent was the way Redditors discussed the short squeeze replicated on Twitter and in digital news?
  2. What potential role do platform affordances play in shaping divergence between Reddit and other digital spaces?
  • Based on these three theoretical pieces, we develop two research questions

Data & Method

Data

  • Number of Texts: 349,039
    • Twitter 256,297
      • all tweets using #GameStop (English, no bots)
      • top 100 selected for manual coding,
    • Reddit 91,321
      • submissions to r/wallstreetbets (filtered with SVM classifier)
      • top 100 selected for manual coding
    • News: 1,421
      • collected with search term “gamestop” from Media Cloud’s “U.S. Mainstream Media”
  • To answer our research question we collected data from 3 sources between 22 January and 11 February 2021
  • the largest is Twitter, where we collected all tweets using the #GameStop hashtag, but removed content written by bots or not in English
  • We collected all submissions to the wallstreetbets subreddit, but trained a machine learning model to only keep the relevant content
  • for news media, we used media cloud’s “U.S. Mainstream Media” collections and scraped all articles using the term gamestop

Method: topic models

  • Implementation: Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) from Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al. 2011)
  • Similar to LDA topic models
  • Produces document-per-topic (\(W\)) and word-per-topic (\(H\)) probabilities
  • While LDA can only use integers, NMF can deal with all non-negative real numbers
  • That means we can weight terms with tf-idf

→ better for shorter texts on and .

  • We use topic models to analyse all 250k texts
  • more specifically we use Non-Negative Matrix Factorization
  • this is in practice very similar to LDA topic models in that it produces a document-per-topic (\(W\)) and a word-per-topic (\(H\)) probabilities matrix
  • The difference is that the algorithm can deal with more than just integers, which makes it possible to weight the input terms, making NMF superior for short texts, such as on the Twitter and Reddit

Method: manual coding

  • We code 100 most “prominent” posts
    • Reddit: prominence = upvotes
    • Twitter: prominence = retweets + likes
  • 4 categories
    • political
    • community-building
    • call for action
    • exclusive language
  • We complement this with a manual content analysis of the 100 most prominent social media posts
  • so for Reddit the most upvoted posts
  • for Twitter we add up retweets and likes to make a simple prominence score
  • We employ four theory drive categories

Results

Topic Models

Fig 1: Prevalence of Topics in the Reddit, Twitter and News models

  • Moving on to our results:
  • We found that calculating one model for each content source makes most sense as the number of texts are so unevenly distributed and the Twitter topics would otherwise dominated everything
  • We used normalized pointwise mutual information (Newman et al. 2010), semantic coherence (Mimno et al. (2011) and exclusivity (Roberts et al. 2014) to find an optimal number of topics
  • we landed on k of 19 for the Reddit corpus, 25 for Twitter and 18 topics for the news corpus.
  • For Reddit, we mostly see coordinating action around the short squeeze itself: dominating topic 5; 13 and 1
  • For Twitter
    • the most salient topics had a greater political focus: 8 or topic 24
    • information about the progress of the short squeeze, topics 4 and 18
  • for news media
    • We get a whole lot of generic economic news
    • And some topics discussng US politics (e.g. topics 4 and 15), and the RobinHood app controversy (topic 13)

(Preliminary) Conclusion

  • Reddit:
    • drove collective (financial) action
    • less influence in shaping understanding of events
  • Twitter:
    • action mobilisation took a backseat
    • emphasised understanding and interpreting events
  • news media:
    • no calls for action
    • high focus on the political context and implications of events
    • omits anti-elitist sentiment of Twitter and Reddit
  • so what we can conclude at this point:
    • there clearly isn’t one social media logic
    • Reddit drove collective financial action but framing of events was not really replicated on other platforms, which made sense of the events on their own, which answer our first research question
    • On Twitter, we see less mobilisation and more emphasis on understanding the events or giving it their own spin
